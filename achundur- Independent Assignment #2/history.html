Why did we start eating fermented foods?

For us, fermentation was a crucial component to “extend the shelf life of perishable food” (Swain 2014, 3). The preservation aspect of fermentation acted as yet another force impacting human’s lifestyle. By safely preserving perishables, we were able to store and eat these foods out of season and in times of scarcity (Campbell-Platt 2003, 630). Based on archaeological evidence, chemicals found in Neolithic vessels indicate that these ancestors had been intentionally fermenting and storing food such as fruit, honey, and rice (Selhub 2014, 1) as early as 10,000 years ago. This safe way of storing perishables was especially useful for survival during the winter season and during periods of drought (Swain 2014, 1).



From Living History Farms: "The milk of camels, goats, sheep, and cattle was naturally fermented as far back as 10,000 BCE. It’s likely that the fermentation occurred spontaneously, rather than intentionally, due to naturally existing microflora present in the milk. It has been suggested that the first yogurts were produced in goat bags draped over the backs of camels in the heat of North Africa, where temperatures around 110°F during the day made ideal conditions for fermentation to occur."



While we developed adaptations from cooking that allowed us to become primarily land-mammals, we simultaneously lost the anatomical features that enabled us to easily climb trees. As a result, we ate fruits that were already on the ground- food that had probably begun to ferment (Peters 13). We had to use different strategies to gather the food that we needed. In general, other animals avoided the “rotting” or older food that had begun to ferment, so with less competition our ancestors turned to these fermented foods as a “fallback” strategy (Carrigan 2014, 1). In turn, there was a “selective pressure that increased the tolerance of dietary alcohol before human-directed fermentation” (Peters 2019, 13).
